My First Stitches

Like many other young girls, I learned to knit from my grandmother. On a whim I finagled my mom into buying a beginner's knitting kit, and it fell to my grandmother to actually teach me. She cast on the first few stitches and taught me how to do a knit stitch which creates a basic garter stitch pattern.

Dishcloths > Scarfs

This is when the inevitable happened; I got bored and stopped. The garter stitch is a fundemental skill for beginners, but a scarf takes a long time to complete and is very, very boring. A better project for absulote beginners is a cotton dishcloth. It is easier to try new stitch patterns and techniques on a short project where the stakes are low if you mess it up. Plus, you get a much quicker dopamine hit for completing something versus slogging along on a scarf for ages.

Avoiding Ladders

Another issue crops up if you drop a stitch (meaning you accidently drop it from your needles). Unless you address it, a dropped stitch will unravel the entire length of your piece. This leaves obvious ladders in the finished product. So a very long project for a new knitter risks heartbreaking mistakes before they know how to fix a dropped stitch. Luckily dropped stitches are fairly easy to fix, but I was very glad to have a basic understanding of knitting before I attempted to correct one for the first time.

So Squishy

Hats have been my go-to project as someone that doesn't enjoy long slogs. Some people are familiar with ribbing in knitwear, but it has variations beyond just knit1, purl1. Fisherman's ribbing effectively knits every row twice (and therefore takes longer to knit), producing ribbing that is delightlfully squishy, thick, and warm. The tradeoff of the extra time spent knitting is offset by how quick hats generally are to make and the extra warmth of the finished product.

Floating with Fair Isle

Because of my penchant for hats, I looked for other, faster ways to make them warm. When knits have more than one color in the same row, typically they are using a using a technique called fair isle. While traditionally there are only two colors present in each row, there is no techinal limit except your own sanity. For each stitch in one color, the other color "floats" behind it, creating a dense and cozy knit.

Everything is on YouTube

YouTube is an irreplaceable resource. There are millions of knitting tutorials for everything under the sun. While I don't do projects involving particulary esoteric techniques, I have yet to be let down by YouTube.

Ravelry is Inevitable

Most peeople that knit long enough will eventually end up with a Ravelry account. It is a database of seemingly every pattern published in the past thirty years. Not only does it have extensive search funtions and let you purchase individual patterns, Ravelry alerts you to any corrections or errata for a pattern. Personally, I find the most useful feature is you can see other people's projects if they make them public. This allows you to see what the pattern looks like on a regular person in normal lighting rather than a model being professionally photographed.
